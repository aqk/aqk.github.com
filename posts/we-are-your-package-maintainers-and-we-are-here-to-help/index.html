We are your package maintainers, and we are here to help

14 May 2020, 00:00

So, I was on my computer, doing normal computer stuff.

Suddenly, a new window reared its head. Whew! It was the Ubuntu Software Updater, here to bring vetted, cryptographically signed updates to my computer, security patches among them. Yay! I had nothing to fear. Or so it would seem.

Being curious, I start poking around. Not long after, I was confronted with this:

Sorry, security people, I should have tagged this post Not Safe Or Secure. You should probably just turn off your computer now.

If any of you with your computer still connected to the Internet are wondering what TLS is, it’s the “S” in HTTPS. It puts the tiny padlock into your browser’s URL bar. Just drag and drop that on anything you need to be secure. It also used to be called SSL. I think HTTPSSL sounds better than HTTPTLS, don’t you? Don’t worry about it, it’s complicated. Computer people are not the best at naming things.

And it definitely doesn’t have any design or implementaion flaws. At All.

All right, but apart from POODLE, BEAST, CRIME, BREACH, Heartbleed, and a few other CVEs, what has the TLS protocol and OpenSSL ever done to us?

I mean, COVID-19 is a good reason to stop doing a host of other things, so you might as well add downgrading hundreds of millions of browsers and other applications to that list! Applications such as, say, package managers. Whoops, not apt. Never mind. (Chris Lamb makes many valid points on that page. But substituting packages are not the only attacks one can launch on an applicaiton delivering other executables.)

If you follow the link above Wait! Don't do it! maybe you are running the downgraded packages right now!!!

… scared you, didn’t I? … Good …

Anyway, if you go ahead and click the link, you will see a “bug report” that is WAY too light on information for my liking.

Especially for a change that re-enables TLS 1.0.

The only post on that page that attempts to justify the roll-back is this:

It’s ok, though, Firefox did it first:

Uh, thanks, Firefox?

(Previous announcement of TLS 1.0 and 1.1 deprecation in Firefox here)

I mean, you could ask why the government doesn’t use a cert from Let’s Encrypt, or just serve pages via plain old non-encrypted HTTP, or expose a Windows 3.11 for Workgroups File Share. The instructions are right on the Internet. Somebody link the government’s Internet department to my blog. If you are sending a FAX, make sure to write the URLs in the margins.

You can certainly ask these things, but I will not, because such pettiness is beneath me. Also, the government knows where I live.

Okay, enough preaching.

What should you do? Well, here’s what I did:

$ apt list glib-networking glib-networking-common glib-networking-services
Listing... Done
glib-networking-common/focal,focal,now 2.64.1-1 all [installed,automatic]
glib-networking-services/focal,now 2.64.1-1 amd64 [installed,automatic]
glib-networking-services/focal 2.64.1-1 i386
glib-networking/focal,now 2.64.1-1 amd64 [installed,automatic]
glib-networking/focal,now 2.64.1-1 i386 [installed,automatic]

Cool. We don’t have the TLS downgrade yet. Let’s keep it that way:

$ sudo apt-mark hold glib-networking glib-networking-common glib-networking-services
glib-networking set on hold.
glib-networking-common set on hold.
glib-networking-services set on hold.

Okay. Now Ubuntu can’t update those packages from under us.

If you have not been keeping up with developments in TLS security, and weren’t terrified of the prospect of TLS 1.0 being turned back on for millions of computer users worldwide, here’s my TL;DR:

The moral of this story is that even on an open source OS, developed in the open, with maintainers that are well-meaning, your computer will be updated by people you don’t know, and change and break in ways you will probably never notice, or be able to fix. But don’t worry about those updates that much, because it’s definitely already broken. And that’s on the well-maintained ones.

I’m betting this TLS-downgrade attack, I mean, “update” was even more opaquely described elsewhere.

Sleep well!
