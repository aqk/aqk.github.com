We are your package maintainers, and we are here to help

14 May 2020, 00:00

So, I was on my computer, doing normal computer stuff.

Suddenly the trusty Ubuntu Software Updater rear its head.

Being curious, I start poking around, and not long after, I was confronted with this:

Sorry, security people, I should have tagged this post Not Safe Or Secure.

If you follow the link above Wait! Don't do it! maybe you are running the updated packages right now!!!

… scared you, didn’t I? … Good …

Anyway, if you go ahead and click the link, you will see a “bug report” that is WAY too light on information for my liking.

Especially for a change that re-enables TLS 1.0.

The only post on that page that attempts to justify the roll-back is this:

It’s ok, Firefox did it first:

Uh, thanks, Firefox?

(Previous announcement of TLS 1.0 and 1.1 deprecation in Firefox here)

I mean, you could ask why the government doesn’t use a cert from Let’s Encrypt, or just serve via plain old non-encrypted HTTP, or expose a Windows 3.11 for Workgroups File Share. The instructions are right on the Internet. But I certainly will not, because such pettiness is beneath me.

Okay, enough preaching.

What should you do? Well, here’s what I did:

$ apt list glib-networking glib-networking-common glib-networking-services
Listing... Done
glib-networking-common/focal,focal,now 2.64.1-1 all [installed,automatic]
glib-networking-services/focal,now 2.64.1-1 amd64 [installed,automatic]
glib-networking-services/focal 2.64.1-1 i386
glib-networking/focal,now 2.64.1-1 amd64 [installed,automatic]
glib-networking/focal,now 2.64.1-1 i386 [installed,automatic]

Cool. We don’t have the TLS downgrade yet. Let’s keep it that way:

$ sudo apt-mark hold glib-networking glib-networking-common glib-networking-services
glib-networking set on hold.
glib-networking-common set on hold.
glib-networking-services set on hold.

Okay. Now Ubuntu can’t update those packages from under us.

If you have not been keeping up with developments in TLS security, and weren’t terrified of the prospect of TLS 1.0 being turned back on for millions of computer users worldwide, here’s the TL;DR:

The moral of this story is that even on an open source OS, developed in the open, with maintainers that are well-meaning, your computer will be updated by people you don’t know, and change and break in ways you have no hope of ever noticing, or being able to fix. But don’t worry about those updates that much, because it’s definitely already broken. And that’s on the well-maintained ones.

I’m betting this TLS-downgrade attack, I mean, “update” was even more opaquely described elsewhere.

Sleep well!
